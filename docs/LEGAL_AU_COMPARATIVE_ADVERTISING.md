## Regulatory framework — Australia

| Regulation | Purpose | Relevant to us |
|---|---|---|
| **Competition and Consumer Act 2010 (Cth), Schedule 2 — Australian Consumer Law** | Federal consumer protection statute | The operative statute |
| **ACL s.18** | Prohibits misleading or deceptive conduct in trade or commerce | Civil-only, but strict — no intent required |
| **ACL s.29** | Prohibits false or misleading representations about goods/services | Criminal + civil penalties; s.29(1)(i) specifically covers price |
| **ACL s.33** | Prohibits misleading conduct as to nature/characteristics of goods | Belt-and-braces alongside s.18/s.29 |
| **Trade Marks Act 1995 (Cth) s.122(1)(f)** | **Comparative advertising defence to TM infringement — this is the correct sub-section, NOT s.122(1)(d) which is descriptive-use defence** | Bedrock defence for naming a competitor |
| **AANA Code of Ethics** (self-regulatory) | Ad Standards Community Panel enforcement | Broader than ACL — covers taste, decency, denigration |
| **Ad Standards Bureau rulings** | Case-by-case complaint adjudication | Precedent for comparative-ad tone |

## Post-November 2022 penalty regime — the biggest globally

The *Treasury Laws Amendment (More Competition, Better Prices) Act 2022* (in force 10 Nov 2022) raised maximum corporate penalties for s.29 (and other Part 3-1 provisions) to the greatest of:

- **AUD $50 million** per contravention, OR
- **3× the value of benefit obtained** from the contravention, OR
- **30% of adjusted turnover** during the breach period.

For a small business, effective penalty is generally the AUD $2.5M maximum. But ACCC has direct enforcement discretion — no court intermediary — via infringement notices.

**Practical impact**: AU is now the single highest-stakes jurisdiction on Earth for misleading comparative advertising. Stale prices, missing dates, unverifiable claims = potential eight-figure exposure.

## What's DEFINITELY allowed

- **Truthful comparative advertising** — long-established at common law and under ACL.
- **Naming competitors** — protected under TMA 1995 (Cth) **s.122(1)(f)** for the purpose of comparative advertising.
- **Displaying competitor prices** — protected under ACL s.18/s.29 if truthful, current, verifiable, like-for-like, and non-misleading in overall presentation.
- **Aggregating public information** — protected.
- **Fair puffery** — subjective, non-falsifiable claims are non-actionable. But less latitude than USA.

## What requires CARE

- **Superlatives ("Australia's best", "cheapest")** — ACCC treats these as objective claims requiring substantiation. *ACCC v TPG Internet* (Federal Court + High Court 2013) — headline price obscured by fine-print was misleading.
- **Feature-tick tables** — must accurately represent competitor capability. Cherry-picking dimensions favourable to us + omitting where they beat us = potential s.18 breach.
- **Fine-print disclaimers** — do NOT cure a misleading overall impression under ACL. *ACCC v TPG*.
- **Implied claims** — the "dominant message" test applies. The ACCC and Federal Court look at overall impression, not literal words.
- **Comparative price claims with hidden conditions** — e.g. "$X/mo" that turns out to be intro pricing = *Optus Business Solutions* ACCC action 2019.

## What is NOT allowed

- **Misleading conduct** under s.18 — no intent required, strict liability for practical purposes.
- **False representations about price** under s.29(1)(i) — including stale pricing that becomes false when the competitor changes theirs.
- **Bait advertising** — s.35 — advertising a specific price for goods you don't intend to supply at that price in reasonable quantities.
- **Denigration** — see AANA Code § 2.1 (fair competition), Ad Standards case law.
- **Deceptive use of a competitor's trade mark** beyond nominative reference — traditional TMA 1995 s.120 infringement.

## Landmark cases

| Case | Court | Lesson |
|---|---|---|
| *ACCC v TPG Internet* [2013] HCA 54 | High Court 2013 | Dominant-message test — fine-print disclaimers cannot cure a misleading overall headline. |
| *ACCC v Coles Supermarkets* [2014] FCA 634 | Federal Court 2014 | "Freshly Baked" bread that was par-baked overseas = misleading representation. Small print didn't save it. |
| *ACCC v Optus Business Solutions* [2019] FCA 1093 | Federal Court 2019 | Small print + fair-averaging couldn't rescue misleading NBN speed claim. |
| *Google Inc v ACCC* [2013] HCA 1 | High Court 2013 | Publisher liability limits (Google's AdWords) — but doesn't help the advertiser (us). |
| *Kraft Foods v Bega Cheese* [2018] FCA 549 | Federal Court 2018 | Comparative advertising via trade-mark use — reinforced TMA s.122(1)(f) as bedrock defence. |
| *ACCC v LG Electronics* [2018] FCA 1096 | Federal Court 2018 | Failure to comply with ACL consumer guarantee representations = $160k penalty × 3 contraventions. |

## Trade Marks Act citation — CRITICAL

**Correct citation:** Trade Marks Act 1995 (Cth) **s.122(1)(f)**.

Verbatim text: *"a person does not infringe a registered trade mark when: [...] the person uses the trade mark for the purposes of comparative advertising."*

**Common misciting error:** s.122(1)(d) is a DIFFERENT defence — good-faith use to indicate kind, quality, quantity, characteristics. Do NOT confuse them.

Both are separate defences; both survive post-2018 *Trade Marks Amendment (Productivity Commission Response Part 1) Act*.

Our AU chart footer correctly cites s.122(1)(f).
